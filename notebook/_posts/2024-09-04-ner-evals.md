<span class="firstcharacter">I</span>t has been a year since I released [calamanCy](https://github.com/ljvmiranda921/calamancy) and its accompanying BERT-based models.
They were pretty good at the time, even surpassing LLM performance ([Miranda, 2023](https://aclanthology.org/2023.sealp-1.2.pdf)).
However, newer versions of GPT were released, and recent LLMs even boast multilingual capabilities.
I guess it's high time to refresh my evals and post them here.

In this blog post, I will be evaluating on the [Tagalog TLUnified-NER](https://huggingface.co/datasets/ljvmiranda921/tlunified-ner) dataset (test size: 782 sentences).
I evaluated on the following models:

- **GPT-4**: for the past year, OpenAI upgraded their GPT-4 models, and they seem to work well in my other multilingual projects. For this blog post, I will use the `gpt-4-turbo-2024-04-09` model for evaluation.

- **Finetuned Tagalog GliNER models**: [GliNER](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.08526) uses bidirectional LMs that allows you to define unseen entity labels at inference time. It's a highly-flexible model and I released GliNER finetunes for Tagalog. I will be evaluating these finetunes and report the best scoring model.

- **Aya-101 13B and Aya-23 8B and 35B**: the Aya model suite is a multilingual LLM that was finetuned on human-written and machine-generated texts. The [Aya-101 model](https://huggingface.co/CohereForAI/aya-101) uses the [Aya Dataset Collection](https://huggingface.co/datasets/CohereForAI/aya_collection_language_split) that includes some Filipino examples. The [Aya-23 models](https://huggingface.co/collections/CohereForAI/c4ai-aya-23-664f4cda3fa1a30553b221dc) don't have Filipino but I'm hoping there's some cross-lingual transfer involved here to see what would happen.

- **Llama 3.1 8B and 70B**: recently, Meta released the Llama 3.1 model suite, claiming strong multilingual performance. I'm not sure what the language provenance of their pretraining and post-training datasets, but they seem to work well on my vibes-based checks.

- **SEA-LION-v2, Sailor, and SeaLLM-v3**: these models are separate efforts from the Southeast Asian community.
  Personally, it's interesting to see which organizations are building these SEA models. I have more thoughts on this but maybe in the future.

  - [SEA-LION v2.1](https://huggingface.co/aisingapore/llama3-8b-cpt-sea-lionv2.1-instruct) is an 8B model built by AI Singapore as a Llama-3 finetune.
  - [SeaLLM-v3](https://huggingface.co/collections/SeaLLMs/seallms-v3-668f3a52e1e6fbaad5752cdb) is a 7B model built by Alibaba and is based on Qwen2. They also employed this Language Specific Neuron training ([Tang et al., 2024](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2402.16438)) in their pretraining paradigm&mdash; interesting!
  - Finally, [Sailor](https://huggingface.co/sail/Sailor-7B-Chat) was built by some private company called Sea and Singapore University of Technology and Design (SUTD) and is based on Qwen1.5.

## Final thoughts: a comprehensive evaluation set for Tagalog

Right now, we're evaluating LLMs on a very specific and highly-structured linguistic task.
An LLM performing well on TLUnified-NER just means it works well on Tagalog NER in general, but does not necessarily mean that it actually works on Tagalog.
To answer that question, we need more comprehensive evaluation sets from a broad range of tasks: translation, instruction-following, general chat, etc.
This might mean creating new datasets or curating existing ones.
